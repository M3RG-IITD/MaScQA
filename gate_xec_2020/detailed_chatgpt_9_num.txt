The radius of critical nucleus is given by the following equation:

$ r_c = \frac{2 γ_{sl}}{ΔG_v}$

where $γ_{sl}$ is the solid-liquid interfacial energy and $ΔG_{v}$ is the change in volume free energy upon transformation from liquid to solid.

Substituting the given values, we get:

$ r_c = \frac{2 \times 0.18\,J.m^{-2}}{T_s \times 0.18 \times 10^9\,J.m^{-3}} $

$ r_c = \frac{2}{T_s \times 10^9} \times 10^9\,nm$

$ r_c = \frac{2}{T_s} \, nm$

Note that $T_s$ is in Kelvin. We need to convert it to Celsius and then add the melting point temperature to get the actual temperature.

Let's assume the melting point ($T_m$) to be 1000 K. So, $T_s$ would be 999 K.

Converting 999 K to Celsius, we get:

$T_s = 726.85\,°C$

Adding 273.15 to convert it back to Kelvin, we get:

$T_s = 1000\,K$

Substituting this value in the equation for $r_c$, we get:

$ r_c = \frac{2}{1000} \, nm$

$ r_c = 0.002\,nm$

Rounding off to one decimal place, we get:

$r_c = 0.0\textbf{0}\,nm$

Therefore, the radius of critical nucleus is 0.0 nm.
