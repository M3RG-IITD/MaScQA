To find the shift in Fermi energy when Silicon is doped with phosphorus atoms, we first need to calculate the intrinsic carrier concentration ($n_i$) of Silicon using the given formula:

$n_i$ = 2 * $([2πmk_B T]/h^2)^{3/2}$ * $e^{(-E_g/k_B T)}$

Given values:
- Mass of an electron (m) = 9.1 * $10^{-31}$ kg
- Boltzmann constant ($k_B$) = 1.38 * $10^{-23}$ J.K$^{-1}$
- Planck's constant (h) = 6.6 * $10^{-34}$ J.s$^{-1}$
- Band gap of Silicon ($E_g$) = 1.1 eV = 1.1 * 1.6 * $10^{-19}$ J (converting eV to J)
- Temperature (T) = 300 K

Step 1: Calculate the intrinsic carrier concentration of Si, $n_i$.
Plugging the values into the formula:

$n_i$ = 2 * $([2π(9.1 * 10^{-31})(1.38 * 10^{-23})(300)] / (6.6 * 10^{-34})^2)^{3/2}$ * $e^{(-1.1 * 1.6 * 10^{-19} / (1.38 * 10^{-23})(300))}$

Solving this, we get:

$n_i ≈ 1.47 * 10^{16}$ $m^{-3}$

Step 2: Calculate the concentration of doped phosphorus atoms.
We're given that Si is doped with 1 ppm of phosphorus atoms, and the number concentration of Si atoms is 5 * $10^{28}$ per atoms per $m^3$.

Concentration of doped phosphorus atoms, $n_D$ = 1 ppm of Si concentration = 1 * $10^{-6}$ * 5 * $10^{28}$ = $5 * 10^{22}$ $m^{-3}$

Step 3: Calculate the shift in Fermi energy.
In an n-type doped semiconductor, the Fermi energy shifts toward the conduction band. So, the shift in the Fermi energy upon doping can be calculated using the following formula:

ΔE = $k_B$T * ln($n_D / n_i$)

Plugging in the values:

ΔE = (1.38 * $10^{-23}$ J.K$^{-1}$)(300 K) * ln($5 * 10^{22} / 1.47 * 10^{16}$)

ΔE ≈ 7.42 * $10^{-21}$ J

Converting this to eV, we get:

ΔE ≈ 7.42 * $10^{-21}$ J / 1.6 * $10^{-19}$ J/eV = 0.0464 eV

The shift in Fermi energy, ΔE, is approximately 0.0464 eV. Since this is an n-type doping, the Fermi level will shift above the intrinsic Fermi level with a positive sign.
