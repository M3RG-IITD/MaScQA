We can use the formula for saturation magnetization of an FCC metal to find the number of Bohr magnetons per atom:

$M_{sat} = \frac{\mu_0 \mu_{B} n(n+2)}{3a^3}$

where $\mu_0$ is the magnetic constant, $\mu_B$ is the Bohr magneton, $n$ is the number of unpaired electrons per atom, and $a$ is the lattice parameter.

Rearranging this equation to solve for $n$ gives:

$n = \sqrt{\frac{3M_{sat}a^3}{\mu_0 \mu_B}} - 2$

Plugging in the given values gives:

$n = \sqrt{\frac{3(600 \text{ kA/m})(0.2 \text{ nm})^3}{4\pi \times 10^{-7} \text{ N/A}^2 \times 9.27 \times 10^{-24} \text{ A m}^2}} - 2$

$n = 2.02$

The net magnetic moment per atom is given by:

$\mu = n\mu_B$

$\mu = (2.02)(9.27 \times 10^{-24} \text{ A m}^2) = 1.87 \times 10^{-23} \text{ A m}^2$

Converting to Bohr magnetons gives:

$\mu = \frac{1.87 \times 10^{-23} \text{ A m}^2}{9.27 \times 10^{-24} \text{ A m}^2} = 2.02$

Therefore, the correct answer is (B) 2.02 * $10^{57}$.
