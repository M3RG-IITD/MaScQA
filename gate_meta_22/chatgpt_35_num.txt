Using the equation for viscosity $\eta$,

$\eta=\frac{\text{shear stress}}{\text{velocity gradient}}$

We can calculate the velocity gradient as follows:

$u=\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta y}\cdot v$

$\Delta x$ is the distance between the plates, $\Delta x=10^{-3}$ m.

$v$ is the velocity of the upper plate, $v=2\ m/s$.

$\Delta y$ is the distance traveled by the upper plate over a time interval $\Delta t$ during which the shear stress $F/A$ is applied to keep it moving with constant velocity. 

$\Delta y = v \cdot \Delta t$

From Newton's second law of motion, the force F on the upper plate is given by:

$F=\frac{A}{\Delta y}\cdot 5\ Pa$

$A$ is the area of the plates, $A=0.01\ m^2$.

Substituting $\Delta y$ and $F$:

$5\ Pa=\frac{A}{v\cdot \Delta t} \cdot \frac{A}{\Delta y}$

$5\ Pa=\frac{A^2}{v \cdot \Delta t \cdot \Delta y}$

Rearranging to solve for $\Delta t$:

$\Delta t = \frac{A^2}{5\ v\ \Delta y}$

Substituting $\Delta t$ into the expression for the velocity gradient gives:

$\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta y} = \frac{v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v \cdot 5\ v\ \Delta y}{A^2}$

$\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta y}=\frac{5\ v^2}{A^2}\cdot\Delta y$

Substituting $\Delta v/\Delta y$ and the given values into the expression for viscosity:

$\eta=\frac{F/A}{\Delta v/\Delta y}$

$\eta=\frac{5\ Pa}{5\ v^2/A^2}=\frac{5\ Pa}{5\cdot 2^2/0.01}\approx 500\ Pa.s$

Therefore, $y=500$.
