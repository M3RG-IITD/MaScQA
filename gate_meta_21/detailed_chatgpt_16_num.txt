We can use the equation:

$$\Delta G° = -nFE°$$

Where $\Delta G°$ is the standard free energy change, $n$ is the number of electrons transferred in the reaction, $F$ is Faraday's constant, and $E°$ is the standard electrode potential.

We know that $n=2$ for the reaction $Ni^{+2} + 2e^{-} \rightarrow Ni$. 

At standard conditions, we have $\Delta G° = -nFE°$, allowing up to find $\Delta G°$:

$$\Delta G° = -2(96500 C \cdot mol^{-1})(-0.25 V) = 48,250 J \cdot mol^{-1}$$

We can also use the equation:

$$\Delta G° = -RT\ln K$$

Where $R$ is the gas constant, $T$ is the temperature, and $K$ is the equilibrium constant. At standard conditions, the equilibrium constant is equal to 1.

Using $R = 8.314 J \cdot mol^{-1} \cdot K^{-1}$ and $T=298 K$, we can solve for $\Delta G°$:

$$\Delta G° = -8.314 J \cdot mol^{-1} \cdot K^{-1} \cdot 298 K \cdot \ln 1= 0 J \cdot mol^{-1}$$

At equilibrium, where $\Delta G° = 0 J \cdot mol^{-1}$, we can find $µ°_{Ni^{+2}}$:

$$\Delta G° = -nFE° = -RT\ln K$$

$$\ln K = \frac{nFE°}{RT}$$

$$K = e^{\frac{nFE°}{RT}}$$

For the reaction $Ni^{+2} + 2e^{-} \rightarrow Ni$, $K$ can be expressed as:

$$K = \frac{[Ni]}{[Ni^{+2}]}$$

Since the standard state of a substance is 1M, we can write:

$$K = \frac{1}{[Ni^{+2}]}$$

Substituting the values we have calculated, we get:

$$\frac{1}{[Ni^{+2}]} = e^{\frac{2(96500 C \cdot mol^{-1})(-0.25 V)}{(8.314 J \cdot mol^{-1} \cdot K^{-1})(298 K)}}$$

Solving this equation for $[Ni^{+2}]$ gives:

$$[Ni^{+2}] = 1.1 \cdot 10^{-6} M$$

Thus, we now have the concentration of $Ni^{+2}$, which allows us to calculate the value of $µ°_{Ni^{+2}}$:

$$µ°_{Ni^{+2}} = µ°_{Ni} + RT\ln [Ni^{+2}]$$

At standard conditions, all elements are in their standard states, which means that $µ°_{Ni} = 0 J \cdot mol^{-1}$. Substituting and solving for $µ°_{Ni^{+2}}$ gives:

$$µ°_{Ni^{+2}} = (8.314 J \cdot mol^{-1} \cdot K^{-1})(298 K)\ln (1.1 \cdot 10^{-6} M) = -60975 J \cdot mol^{-1}$$

Rounding off to the nearest integer, we get the final answer:

Answer: [-60975]
